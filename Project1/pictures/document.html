Design process and rationale


This project is using current data-visualization techniques to recreate Dr. John Snow's map of London's 1854 cholera epidemic. The key point of this map is to reveal the significant correlation between contaminative water and cholera, which demonstrated that the pollution of the water pump caused a horrible disease. Therefore, the evidence shown on the data-visualized map can be persuasive to call for the relevant actions to save people’s life.


The steps to design this project:


  1. Analysis of the audience;
  2. Analysis of the dataset;
  3. Choose the right charts, channels, and other materials;
  4. Select a software program;
  5. Iterative Design process during developing;
  6. Shared projects with others.

The audience of this project includes the people who know about this story and the people who never heard about it. It is necessary to contain a document file with short introduction.


The second step is to go through carefully the dataset files. There are three files providing different information for this project


  • Streets.json: provide the coordination of streets to generate John Snow’s Map;
  • Pumps: provide the water pumps coordinates;
  • Deathdays: provide the death date and deaths on that day;
  • Death_age_sex: provide demographic information of the deaths.


My initial design sketch (see picture1) consists of a John Snow’s map displaying the location of the water pump and the people's death location. I planned to add a bar chart to show the death number along with the time went by.


According to history, the death number increased suddenly in a short time and should decrease quickly after the particular day in which the government moved the water pump. Therefore the bar chart should show this tendency.

Death points color and shape

To distinguish the different age groups, I used the gradient ramp generated by the ColorBrewer2. The color is selected because the red color reminds people to think of the blood and the brighter yellow can represent life. I chose the dark red, the color of blood, as the color of the bar chart because its value represents the death number of people per day.


Pump shape

Based on the real water pump in the London street in history, I select a water-pump icon to indicate the water pump location on the map. My selection is based on its shape and color. Compared to my map background color, the icon that I chose has distinguished color from map and denotes they are pump intuitive.


Buildings color and street name

Green, the color of life, I used green color to denotes the two exceptional cases that led Snow to isolate the cause of the cholera to the Broad Street pump.
For instance, of the 530 inmates of the Poland Street workhouse, which was only round the corner, only five people had contracted cholera; but no one from the workhouse drank the pump water, for the building had its own well. Among the 70 workers in a Broad Street brewery, where the men were given an allowance of free beer every day and so never drank water at all, there were no fatalities at all.

Questions & Findings


Q1. Was the death really caused by water pollution?

From John Snow’s map, we can find a clear correlation between death and water pump(see below picture). The dead were concentrated near water pumps.



Q2. What age group was dying the most?

The pie chart shows the proportional distribution of age groups. The largest portion of the dead population is young children less than 10 years and old people above 60-year-old. It is reasonable because older people and children have weaker immune systems.
